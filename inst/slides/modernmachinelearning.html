Pruning

  • Trees can easily overfit the data when they grow too large (high variance)

  • Pruning: cut back the tree to reduce variance

    • Cost-complexity pruning: trade-off between fit and complexity (penalty on size)
    • Cross-validation to choose optimal size
    • Early stopping

Ensemble methods

  • Multiple models can be fit to solve the same problem

  • One can try to choose the best among the models

  • One can also try to combine the models : \(\boldsymbol{\Rightarrow}\) Ensemble

  • Often a combination will perform better than the best single model

  • Base earners should be accurate and diverse

  • Leads to stacking, boosting and bagging

Random forrests

  • Introduced by Leo Breiman
  • A Random Forest is an ensemble of decision trees.
  • Each tree is grown on a bootstrap sample of the data (i.e. bagged trees).
  • At each split, only a random subset of predictors is considered.
  • Final prediction: average across trees

Why Random Forests?

  • Variance reduction
    • Single trees overfit easily, Averaging many different trees reduces variance
  • Built-in nonlinearity & interactions
    • No need to specify transformations or interaction terms, Interactions are learned automatically
  • Minimal modeling assumptions
    • No linearity or distributional assumption

Introduction to Boosting (I)

  • Meta-algorithm to combine a number of ‘weak learners’ (e.g. small trees) into a strong learner that performs better
  • The idea: each new model focusses on the errors made by the previous one
    • Learners added sequentially
    • more focused on difficult observations (i.e. high loss)
  • At the end of the sequence all trees are combined
    • The final model is a weighted sum of all learners

Boosting isn’t a single algorithm — it’s a strategy. We can implement this idea in different ways:

AdaBoost: adjusts sample weights after each learner.

Gradient Boosting: fits the next learner to the residuals or gradients of the loss. Both share the same spirit — focus on what the current model gets wrong — but the mechanism differs. Be caseful to explain there are two types of weights here FROM IBM

Adaptive boosting or AdaBoost: Yoav Freund and Robert Schapire are credited with the creation of the AdaBoost algorithm. This method operates iteratively, identifying misclassified data points and adjusting their weights to minimize the training error. The model continues optimize in a sequential fashion until it yields the strongest predictor.

Gradient boosting: Building on the work of Leo Breiman, Jerome H. Friedman developed gradient boosting, which works by sequentially adding predictors to an ensemble with each one correcting for the errors of its predecessor. However, instead of changing weights of data points like AdaBoost, the gradient boosting trains on the residual errors of the previous predictor. The name, gradient boosting, is used since it combines the gradient descent algorithm and boosting method.

Extreme gradient boosting or XGBoost: XGBoost is an implementation of gradient boosting that’s designed for computational speed and scale. XGBoost leverages multiple cores on the CPU, allowing for learning to occur in parallel during training.

Introduction to Boosting (II)

Why ‘Gradient’ Boosting

  • In gradient descent the derivative of the loss wrt to the parameters is used to adjust parameter values

  • In gradient boosting the derivative of the loss wrt to the predicted values{color=“#b281e2”} is used to fit the next learner

    • We want to change the predictions in the direction that reduces the loss the most
    • Each new learner is fit to the negatieve gradient (i.e. the ‘residual errors’) of the previous learners

In standard gradient descent, we update parameters using the gradient of the loss. In gradient boosting, we don’t have parameters — we have a function F(x). So we take a step in function space: add a new tree that predicts the negative gradient of the loss.

What is the Super Learner?

  • An ensemble learning method that combines multiple algorithms
    (e.g., logistic regression, random forest, xgboost, neural networks)
  • It uses cross-validation to find the optimal weighted combination
    of their predictions.

Instead of choosing a single best model,
the Super Learner learns how to mix them for optimal predictive performance.

How it works

  1. Train base learners on training data
    (e.g., GLM, random forest, xgboost, kNN)
  2. Generate cross-validated predictions for each algorithm
  3. Fit a meta-learner (often a simple regression)
    to find the best weights for combining those predictions
  4. Final model = weighted average of base learners

Interpretable AI

“A model that performs well but cannot be understood is of limited use.”

Sometimes we do not want this simple / gaming the system / low stakes

Many machine learning models (e.g., XGBoost, neural nets) achieve high accuracy but offer little transparency.

In medicine, we need to understand predictions because:

Clinical decisions affect real people.

We need to justify model outputs to clinicians, regulators, and patients.

Models may fail silently in subpopulations.

Diagnostic support systems (e.g., predicting heart failure from EHR data) Which variables (age, lab values, ECG metrics) drive the prediction Helps clinicians verify the model uses clinically plausible patterns Risk prediction models (e.g., 30-day readmission, sepsis onset) Which features increase risk for a given patient Supports individualized counseling and triage Screening prioritization (e.g., cancer detection algorithms) Why the model flags certain images or patients Detects spurious correlations (e.g., scanner artifacts, site bias) Drug safety / pharmacovigilance Which patient factors predict adverse events Ensures fairness across demographics Resource allocation (e.g., ICU admission models) What factors influence model recommendations Important for ethical and legal accountability Genomic / biomarker models Which genes or biomarkers contribute most to predictions Generates hypotheses for biomedical research (but not causality)

Why interpretability?

  • Machine learning models (e.g. random forests, gradient boosting, neural nets) often act as black boxes
  • In medicine, we need to know:
    • Why a model made a decision
    • Which variables contributed most
    • How reliable the prediction is
  • Goals:
    • Trust (Stakeholders: Medical comunity / Patients / Auditors)
    • Improvement
    • Insights
  • Model transparency
  • Post-hoc explanation
    • Local interpretability
    • Global interpretability

Interpretability does not mean Causality



  • It’s about understanding model behavior, not proving what caused an outcome

ICE (Individual Conditional Expectation)

  • For one observation at a time:
    • vary one covariate across grid keeping all other covariates fixed at that person’s values.
  • You get many curves (one per obs.), which shows heterogeneity / interactions:
    • if curves differ a lot, the effect depends on other features.
  • Also called Ceterus paribus plots

PDP (Partial Dependence Plot)

  • The average of the ICE curves over all observations

  • Easier to read (one curve), but can hide heterogeneity

  • Can been seen as misleading when features are strongly correlated

    • because it averages over unrealistic feature combinations

ALE (Accumulated Local Effects)

  • Estimates effects locally:

  • looks at how predictions change when you move a feature a small amount within the data distribution,

  • accumulates these local changes

  • Avoids extrapolating into “impossible” combinations

LIME: Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations

Idea

  • Pick one prediction (one patient)
  • Generate many perturbed versions of that input
  • Observe how the model output changes
  • Fit a simple linear model around that neighborhood

SHapley Additive Explanations

  • Based on cooperative game theory
  • Each covariate is treated as a player in a game:
    • The payout is the model’s prediction
    • The contribution of each player (covariate) is its Shapley value

Properties of Shapley Values

  • Efficiency (add up from baseline to prediction)

  • Symmetry (equal contribution = equal value)

  • Linearity (additive for combined models)

  • Dummy (no effect = zero value)

  • The only value that meets these axioms is the Shaply value

Calculation

  • For each feature, consider all possible subsets of features that exclude it
  • For each subset, compute the model prediction with and without the feature
  • The Shapley value is the weighed average marginal contribution of the feature across all subsets
  • SHAP uses approximations to make this computationally feasible

  • Which features matter most overall

  • How their values push predictions up or down

Epidemiological principles in Machine learning: Pitfalls

Take-home message:




Machine learning techniques can be a useful addition to an epidemiologists tool belt, however it is no substitute for sound study design

Repeated from start

Reading a ML paper

  • When critically reading a paper you pay attention to
    • Does the paper answer an interesting question?
    • Are all aspects of the study described properly
    • Was the study design appropriate?
    • Does the sample allow generalization to the target population?
    • Is the model appropriate?
    • Are variables measured reliably and completely?
    • Do we have good model performance ?
    • Issues with model validity ?
  • All still relevant when we use machine learning !

First there has to be an interesting question to answer. Often the question is not clearly stated. Often in ML papers the question will be to predict some phenomenon. For this to be interesting we should also be able to use this predictions (for example not using variables that would not be available in practice ) and the predictions should be better than those that are already avalable. A special case of using variables that would not be available is target leakage when a variable is used that is linked strongly to the outcome but would not be useful in practice

It is important to be able to generalize from the sample to the population. Hence it is important to know how the sample was selected. In case control studies it is important that they are from the same population and are treated in the same way

Often variables are missing. Excluding incomplete cases may be inefficient (and bias analysis). However missingness may also occur when we want to apply the model on new individuals. Furthermore the missingness itself may provide information. Also errors in the variables may result in bias (which may not always be relevant in a prediction setting) but one should carefully consider how this affect the application of the model

Of course good evaluation of the model is needed. Look at calibration, discrimination and propper validation

All these things do not change when we use ML. However there are a few aspects that we will highlight. We will also look at some case studies:

Generalizability of data I


  • Huge volumes of data brought together to use ML (Big Data)
    • More is not always better
    • Sometimes of questionable quality
    • Data often from different setting than that in which we want to use the model
    • Frankenstein data sets
    • ‘Cases’ and ‘Controls’ often have different sources (check that they are from the same study base).
With frankenstein data sets a data set is used that has been assembled from many different sources without really looking to how appropriate this is. Also often containing overlap.

Case study I

  • Example: New machine learning method for image-based diagnosis of COVID-19
  • Compare lung-images of COVID patients with pneumonia patients
    • All controls were from pediatric patients
  • Often seen in a systematic review of COVID-19 prediction models

Validation

  • Validation essential in ML
  • Honest reporting of model performance
  • Avoid over-fitting
  • Cross validation can be ruined by data leakage
Due to the flexebility of the models good validation of models in ML is even more important than in traditional statistical methods (also because we cannot rely on theoretical derivations of the model properties as much)

Data leakage

  • Data leakage means that some information of the test set is being used when building the model

    • Multiple imputation being done on all data
    • Variable selection on all data
    • Normalizing on all data
    • Data from the same subject should not be present in different fold
    • Do not use information from the test setcolor=“#DB3324”}

Common statistical problems

  • Correlated data
    • clustered, longitudinal, spatial
    • Might not always be a problem but beware of data leakage
  • Censoring / truncation / competing risks
  • Case-control (or other) ‘special’ designs

Propper investigation of model performance


  • Should look at a proper scoring rule
    • E.g.: Brier Score or logistic-loss
    • When n is large enough impossible to beat the ‘true’ model
  • Investigate model calibration
  • Compare to a meaningful baseline model
  • Provide uncertainty of key metrics (still rarely done but required in e.g. TRIPOD+AI)
Do not just report percentage correct or Sens and Spec

Handling of missing data


  • Be careful how missing data is handled in the model
  • Often just complete cases are analysed
    • Gives a selected sample
    • Would only be applicable to subjects that have no missing data in production setting
  • Best way of handling missing data for prediction not the same as for attribution / estimation
  • Pay attention to discrepancies between handling missing data when training and when model is deployed
See for example Sperrin et al 2020

Causal inference (I)




  • Often treatments are included as features to explain an outcome
  • Can we show there is an effect of treatment?
  • Can we make any causal claims?

Causal inference (II)

  • Be careful with standard ML methods
  • Start with DAG
  • Standard machine learning focuses on prediction not estimation/attribution
  • Weak predictors might be dismissed even when confounding
  • Good performance for prediction does not mean good performance for estimation or inference (of causal parameters)
  • Standard techniques are focused on solving bias-variance trade-off for prediction, not for the causal estimand
  • Treatment effect not directly observable
    • Individual prediction error cannot be estimated by comparing predictions and observed outcomes in test set


  • Many developments in causal machine learning

Case-study: Pallative Care (II)


The prediction date for positive cases must meet all the following constraints:

  • The prediction date must be a recorded date of encounter.
  • The prediction date must be at least 3 months prior to date of death (otherwise death is too near).
  • The prediction date can be at most 12 months prior to date of death (otherwise death is too far).
  • The prediction date must be at least 12 months after the date of first encounter (otherwise the patient lacks sufficient history on which to base a prediction).

Case-study: Pallative Care (III)


For negative cases (patients without a date of death), we require that the patient was alive for at least 12 months from the prediction date. We choose the prediction date such that it satisfies all the following constraints:

  • The prediction date must be a recorded date of encounter.
  • The prediction date must be at least 12 months prior to date of last encounter (to avoid ambiguity of death after date of EHR snapshot).
  • The prediction date must be at least 12 months after the date of first encounter (otherwise insufficient history).
