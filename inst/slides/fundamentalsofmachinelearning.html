What is epidemiology

“Study of how often diseases occur in different groups of people and why”


  • In order to do this an epidemiologist needs a sound understanding of:
    • Study design
    • Handling of confounding
    • Data analysis
    • Uncertainty

There are various defenitions of epidemiology. Most are somewhat like the one I have on this slide: “Study of how often diseases occur in different groups of people and why”. So an epidemiology is not just doing the bookkeeping and counting the number of patients. He / she also wants to know:

  • how large the differences are between groups
  • what causes the disease (and probably how to prevent it).
So how can you do that. Set up a study. Avoid biases. Handle confounding. Minimize noise. Analyse the data. We should asses the role of chance so say something about the uncertainty of our estimates. This is where statistics come in.

First let’s look at the history. Statistics traces its origins to probability theory. And from here things like the normal distribution and law of large numbers were developed.

Modern statistics probably started with Karl Pearson. Interested in the geometrical representation of statistical data.Which curve best represents the relation between a covariate and the response. Developed a statistical test to see if sampled points could come from a population described by the line. Another researcher who laid the foundations of modern statistics was Ronald Fisher. He worked on small sample statistics and experimental design. He laid out the fundamentals about the theory on maximum likelihood and statistical tests. Neyman also worked on statistical tests but is best known for the theory of confidence intervals.

Since computers where first invented people were fascinated by these electronic brains. Since this time the question was can these machine perform ‘intelligent’ tasks by just following simple instructions. In 1955 John McCarthy organized a workshop to make machines solve all kinds of problems that were previously reserved for humans. This workshop is now seen as the having laid the foundations of the field of artificial intelligence. Many attendees did groundbreaking work in the early days of AI. Two Cultures

  • Breiman (2001) “Statististical Modelling: The Two Cultures” Statistical Science
    • Data Modelling
    • Algorithmic Modelling

n his influential 2001 paper “Statistical Modeling: The Two Cultures”, Leo Breiman defined data modeling (also called the data model approach) as the traditional statistical culture in which:

“The analysis starts with assuming a stochastic data model for the data, e.g., that the data are generated by a particular family of probability distributions.”

In this framework, the statistician:

Posits a model — for example, a linear regression, logistic regression, or Cox proportional hazards model — that describes how the outcome Y Y depends on the inputs X X.

Estimates parameters of that model (like regression coefficients).

Assesses goodness of fit and interprets the parameters as reflecting aspects of the data-generating process.

Breiman summarized the data modeling culture as one that focuses on inferring the mechanism that generated the data, not necessarily on predictive accuracy. He contrasted it with the algorithmic modeling culture, which treats the data mechanism as unknown and focuses instead on predicting Y Y from X X using flexible algorithms (e.g., random forests, boosting, neural nets).

Here’s a concise quote from the paper (Breiman, 2001):

“In the data modeling culture, a model is assumed to exist, for instance, a relationship between the response Y Y and the predictors X X given by Y=f(X)+ϵ Y=f(X)+ϵ. The statistician uses the data to estimate the parameters in the model and to evaluate how well the model fits the data.”


  • Data Modelling
    • makes strong assumptions
    • Problems with using many covariates
    • Focus on (clinically) interpretable quantities
    • Provides an insight on how nature works

  • Data Modelling
    • makes strong assumptions
    • validity of model not often questioned
    • alternative models may exist that fit the data equaly well but would lead to different conclusions
    • problems with using many covariates

Supervised vs Unsupervised learning (I)


  • Another distinction that is often made is that between supervised and unsupervised learning
    • In supervised learning we want to find a function that maps the inputs (x) to the output (y)
    • In unsupervised learning we do not have an outcome. Here we look at the data that we have and try to find some structure.
  • SL is basically what is called in regression in statistics.
  • In supervised learning problems we have pairs of input (x) and output data (y), also called the labels. Using training data we want to find a function that maps the input to the output. For new examples we ar then able to predict the outcome using only the inputs. In the remainder of this course we will mainly focus on supervised learning problems.

Mention online-learning and reinforcement learning?

(concentrate on supervised learning / regression problems)

  • We have a sample of observations but want to generalize to a population: That is we want to learn rules on how we can combine inputs to create accurate predictions for data we have not seen before

NB: Sometimes also semi-supervised learning, online learning and reenforcement learning are added, we leave them out for simplicity

Training your model(I)

  • We want the predictions to be close to the observed outcomes. What exactly is ‘close’ is defined by a loss function (also called error). Popular loss functions: squared error, logistic loss

  • The cost is the sum of the loss over all examples

The loss function considers the type of model but also considers: * computational aspects * statistical aspects (robustness) * purpose of the model * interpretability

Training your model(II)

  • Training your model means minimizing cost
    • Usually this involves an iterative method


Gradient Descent

  • A popular optimization algorithm is gradient descent
    • At each iteration find the direction in which we can reduce the loss the most (mathematically this is done by calculating the gradient)
    • Take a step in this direction (the size of the step is controlled by the learning rate)
  • Popular improvements of Gradient Descent often use momentum and splitting in (mini-) batches

Evalutation metrics

  • Important to evaluate how well a model performs
  • Different metrics can be computed that highlight different aspects of model performance

Calibration

  • It is important that a model is well calibrated:
    • predicted probabilities should correspond to observed frequencies
    • Calibration-in-the-large: on average predicted probabilities should equal observed frequencies
    • Calibration slope: predictions should not be too extreme or too moderate
      • Can be assessed visually (calibration plot) or using statistics (e.g. Hosmer-Lemeshow test)
      • Regress observed outcomes on predicted probabilities (ideal slope = 1)

Measures of discrimination

  • Confusion matrix: Look at the 2 by 2 classification table of observed vs predicted classes for a certain threshold (e.g. 0.5)

  • sensitivity: fraction correctly classified among \(Y=1\)

  • specificity: fraction correctly classified among \(Y=0\)

ROC curve

  • The ROC curve (receiver operating characteristic) is the sensitivity plotted against (1-specificity) for all values of c.
  • The area under the ROC curve (AUC) is a measure of discrimination
  • Probability randomly selected ‘event’ has higher assigned risk than randomly selected ‘non-event’

Other metrics for overall performance

  • For continuous outcomes: Mean Squared Error (MSE), Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), (Adjusted) \(R^2\)
  • For binary outcomes: Adjusted Log-loss (deviance), Brier score, various pseudo-\(R^2\) measures

Overfitting (III)

Bias Variance trade-off (I)

  • The prediction error consists of two components: bias and variance.
  • Here variance can be seen as the how the predictions change if it would be used on different data-sets. Variance will be higher when the model is very flexible
  • Bias in this context means a systematic error in our predictions as a result of of approximating the complex reality by a (simpler) model
In this context Bias means the inability of the model to fit the data (even if we would have all data). Usually by some assumptions/restrictions that are not met.

Bias Variance trade-off II

  • If model complexity is increased (e.g. by increasing #parameters)
    • Bias will generally decrease but
    • Variance will increase
  • Usually there is a middle ground between too simple and overly complex models so that the error is minimized

Data splitting

  • Some data may be set apart to get an estimate of the error in new observations. (hold-out set, validation set)
  • Disadvantages:
    • Estimate of test-error may be highly variable
    • Data in the validation-set not used in training

validation data is ideally used to train model after validation has been performed. Now error may be overestimated as the model that was validated did not contain all data

Cross-validation

  • Data is split in a number of folds
  • For all folds we do the following:
    • Model is trained on the other folds
    • We evaluate it on the fold we set aside and calculate the error
  • Error is averaged over all folds

  • Alternative method: bootstrap

Data leakage

  • Data leakage means that some information of the test set is being used when building the model

    • Multiple imputation being done on all data
    • Variable selection on all data
    • Normalizing on all data
    • Data from the same subject should not be present in different fold
    • Do not use information from the test set

Regularization

  • Regularization is a way to reduce the complexity of models to avoid overfitting
  • Example: In a logistic regression model the values of the fitted parameters can take extreme values when the model is over-fitted.
  • Solution: Penalize models with large values of the coefficients. This leads to ridge regression and lasso-regression

Ridge Regression (L2 penalty)

\[ \text{Penalty}(\beta) = \sum_j \beta_j^2 \]

Properties: - Shrinks coefficients towards zero (but not exactly zero)
- Keeps all predictors in the model
- Useful when predictors are highly correlated
- Reduces variance, improves generalization

Ridge Geometry (intuition)

  • Constraint region: circle (L2 norm)
  • Shrinkage distributes across correlated predictors
  • Good for stabilizing estimates, not for variable selection

Lasso Regression (L1 penalty)

\[ \text{Penalty}(\beta) = \sum_j \|\beta_j\| \]

Properties: - Shrinks some coefficients exactly to zero - Performs variable selection - Leads to sparse models (easier interpretation) - May be unstable when predictors are highly correlated

Lasso Geometry (intuition)

  • Constraint region: diamond (L1 norm)
  • Corners correspond to zero coefficients
  • Encourages sparsity

Elastic Net (Hybrid Approach)

\[ \text{Penalty} = \alpha \sum \|\beta\_j\| + (1-\alpha) \sum \beta\_j^2 \]

  • Combines strengths of ridge and lasso
  • Useful when predictors are highly correlated
  • Tuned by two parameters:
    • \(\lambda\): overall penalty strength
    • \(\alpha\): mix between L1 and L2

Hyper parameters (I)

  • Best penalty should be found that balances bias and variance
    • Example of a hyper-parameter
  • Many other algorithms also involve so called hyper-parameters.
    • Parameters to control the learning process
  • In classical statistical models think of:
    • Order of polynomial effects/splines
    • Include interactions (Yes/No)

Hyper parameters (II)

  • In general: optimal value of the hyper-parameters not derived directly in training of models
  • Instead a hyper-parameter search is carried out (hyper-parameter tuning).
  • One strategy:
    • create a grid of hyper parameter values and
    • train the model for each.
  • Best value is for which the prediction error is lowest
  • For a fair comparison data splitting or (nested) cross validation is needed

P-splines I

  • Suppose we want to model a smooth curve ( f(x) ) instead of a straight line.
  • We can approximate ( f(x) ) by a B-spline basis:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K \beta_k B_k(x) \]

  • To prevent overfitting with too many basis functions, we penalize the roughness of the coefficients: \[ \min_{\beta} \sum_i (y_i - f(x_i))^2 + \lambda \sum_j (\Delta^d \beta_j)^2 \]

  • where \(\Delta^d \beta_j\) are differences between neighboring coefficients.

tidymodels: a modeling framework

  • tidymodels is not a single package, but a collection of packages
  • Goal: a consistent, principled workflow for statistical modeling and machine learning
  • Key design principles:
    • Separation of model specification, preprocessing, and evaluation
    • Tidy data structures (data frames, tibbles)
    • Explicit handling of resampling and tuning

Key idea:
tidymodels makes modeling decisions explicit and reproducible.

Model specification ≠ model implementation

  • Models are defined at a statistical level:
    • logistic_reg()
    • linear_reg()
    • mlp()
    • rand_forest()
  • The computational backend is chosen separately:
    • glm, glmnet, ranger, nnet, xgboost, etc

Preprocessing is part of the model

  • Preprocessing steps (e.g. scaling, imputation, splines):
    • Can affect predictions
    • Must be learned only from training data
  • In tidymodels:
    • Preprocessing is defined in a recipe
    • Applied consistently across resamples and test sets
    • prep: estimates parameters, bake applies to data
  • Recipes and models are combined in a workflow

Tuning and resampling

  • rsample is used for data splitting and resampling (cross-validation, bootstrapping)
  • Different performance metrics can be computed with yardstick
  • Hyperparameters are tuned using tune() in the specification of the model and using the dials package

tidymodels philosophy

  • Make modelling easier by providing a common interface
  • Encourage good modelling practices
  • Adhere to tidyverse principles
    • Human centric
    • Consistent / Predictable
    • Composable
    • Encourage use of the pipe and functional programming
